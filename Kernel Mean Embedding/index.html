KERNEL MEAN EMBEDDING

Welcome to my first-ever blog post! No better way to start off than with an attempt to explain Kernel Mean Embedding. This post should be accessible to as many people from different sorts of backgrounds as possible. Don't let the theory scare you as this will become much clearer with the toy examples. These examples are written in Julia, which many of you won't be familiar with. However, Julia's code is very easy to interpret! The outline of the theory is based on the review article of Kernel Mean Embedding by Muandet et al. Kernel Methods

Consider some linearly separatable data which can be evaluated by the classical machine learning algorithms perceptron, support vector machine and principal component analysis. These algorithms consider the data, x,x′∈Xx,x' \in \mathcal Xx,x′∈X, with X\mathcal XX a non empty set, through their inner product ⟨x,x′⟩\langle x, x' \rangle⟨x,x′⟩. Another way to view this inner product is as a linear similarity measure between the elements of X\mathcal XX. But what if the data is more complex and cannot be linearly evaluated?

Let's consider this arbitrary classification problem, in which two data circles cannot be linearly separated.

Here the kernel methods come into play! Kernel methods replace the inner product ⟨x,x′⟩\langle x, x' \rangle⟨x,x′⟩ as a similarity measure with a non-linear one. Consider the non-linear transformation:

ϕ:X→Fx↦ϕ(x) \phi:\mathcal X\to \mathcal F \\ x \mapsto \phi (x) ϕ:X→Fx↦ϕ(x)

from X\mathcal XX to the high-dimensional feature space F\mathcal FF. In this new feature space the inner product can be evaluated:

k(x,x′)=⟨ϕ(x),ϕ(x)′⟩F, k(x,x') = \langle \phi(x), \phi(x)' \rangle_{\mathcal{F}}, k(x,x′)=⟨ϕ(x),ϕ(x)′⟩F​,
