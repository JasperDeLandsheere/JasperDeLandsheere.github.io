KERNEL MEAN EMBEDDING

This post about Kernel Mean Embedding is an excerpt from my thesis on using Kernel Mean Embedding to predict Pharmaceutical granules' size, changed slightly to fit a more broader audience. This post should be accessible to as many people from different sorts of backgrounds as possible. Don't let the theory scare you as this will become much clearer with the toy examples. These examples are written in Julia, which many of you won't be familiar with. However, Julia's code is very easy to interpret! The outline of the theory is based on the review article of Kernel Mean Embedding by Muandet et al. Finally, the Pluto notebooks can be found on my Github and all of my sources are referenced at the bottom of this page. I also hope to upload my thesis' version of this part somewhere in July. Enjoy!

Kernel Methods

Inner products serve as a powerful tool in many established machine learning algorithms, such as principal component analysis (PCA)[1] [2], perceptron [3] [4], and support vector machine (SVM) [5]. These algorithms consider the data, e.g., x,x′∈X\mathbf{x},\mathbf{x}' \in \mathcal Xx,x′∈X, with X\mathcal XX a non empty set, through their inner product ⟨x,x′⟩\langle \mathbf{x}, \mathbf{x}' \rangle⟨x,x′⟩, which can be interpreted as a similarity measure between x\mathbf{x}x and x′\mathbf{x}'x′. But real-life data is often complex and the class of linear functions induced by the inner products might prove to be insufficient. The aim of kernel methods is to handle complex data which can't be linearly evaluated, by replacing ⟨x,x′⟩\langle \mathbf{x}, \mathbf{x}' \rangle⟨x,x′⟩ with some other (non-linear) similarity measure.

Naturally, an extension of ⟨x,x′⟩\langle \mathbf{x}, \mathbf{x}' \rangle⟨x,x′⟩ can be made by explicitly applying a non-linear transformation:

ϕ:X→F,x↦ϕ(x), \phi:\mathcal X\to \mathcal F,\\ x \mapsto \phi (x), ϕ:X→F,x↦ϕ(x),

from X\mathcal XX to the high-dimensional feature space F\mathcal FF. In this new feature space the inner product can be evaluated:

k(x,x′):=⟨ϕ(x),ϕ(x′)⟩F, k(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{x}') := \langle \phi(\mathbf{x}), \phi(\mathbf{x}') \rangle_{\mathcal{F}}, k(x,x′):=⟨ϕ(x),ϕ(x′)⟩F​,

where ⟨⋅,⋅⟩F\langle \cdot, \cdot \rangle_{\mathcal{F}}⟨⋅,⋅⟩F​ is the inner product of F\mathcal FF and ϕ\phiϕ is called the feature map. kkk is the kernel function which defines a non-linear similarity measure between x\mathbf{x}x and x′\mathbf{x}'x′. By substituting ⟨x,x′⟩\langle \mathbf{x}, \mathbf{x}' \rangle⟨x,x′⟩ with ⟨ϕ(x),ϕ(x′)⟩F\langle \phi(\mathbf{x}), \phi(\mathbf{x}') \rangle_{\mathcal{F}}⟨ϕ(x),ϕ(x′)⟩F​ a non-linear extension of the algorithms that consider data through ⟨x,x′⟩\langle \mathbf{x}, \mathbf{x}' \rangle⟨x,x′⟩, can be made. This means a linear algorithm in F\mathcal FF corresponds to a non-linear counterpart in the original input space. It is important to note that the algorithm stays the same, only the space in which the algorithm operates, changes.

The core idea of kernel methods is taking data which lives in an input space where it's not easy to perform machine learning and transform this data to a higher dimensional space where effective use of learning algorithms can be made.

As seen in the above example, to evaluate equation 2 one needs to work in two steps: i) explicitly constructing the feature maps ϕ(x)\phi(\mathbf{x})ϕ(x), and ii) evaluating the inner product ⟨ϕ(x),ϕ(x′)⟩F\langle \phi(\mathbf{x}), \phi(\mathbf{x}') \rangle_{\mathcal{F}}⟨ϕ(x),ϕ(x′)⟩F​. This can become a problem when ϕ(x)\phi(\mathbf{x})ϕ(x) defines a computationally expensive transformation to a high-dimensional featue space. Fortunately, there exists a solution to this by evaluating ⟨ϕ(x),ϕ(x′)⟩F\langle \phi(\mathbf{x}), \phi(\mathbf{x}') \rangle_{\mathcal{F}}⟨ϕ(x),ϕ(x′)⟩F​ without explicitly constructing the feature maps. This is a core idea of kernel methods and is often called "the kernel trick" in the machine learning community. A visual representation of the kernel trick is illustrated below. For the above mentioned example one can just consider k(x,x′)=⟨x,x′⟩2k(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{x}') = {\langle \mathbf{x}, \mathbf{x}' \rangle}^2k(x,x′)=⟨x,x′⟩2 rather than calculating the feature maps explicitly.

Now let's discuss an important requirement needed in order for the kernel trick to do its magic: kkk has to be positive definite. What does this mean? First we have to define the n×nn \times nn×n-Gram or kernel matrix Kij:=k(xi,xj)K_{ij} := k(x_{i},x_{j})Kij​:=k(xi​,xj​), the collection of all pairwise inner products within the set of data vectors xxx.

A symmetric function kkk is positive definite on X\mathcal XX if the Gram matrix is positive definite, that is,

∑i=1n∑j=1jcicjk(xi,xj)≥0,∀xi∈X \displaystyle\sum_{i=1}^{n}\displaystyle\sum_{j=1}^{j} c_{i}c_{j}k(x_{i},x_{j}) \geq 0, \forall x_{i} \in \mathcal X i=1∑n​j=1∑j​ci​cj​k(xi​,xj​)≥0,∀xi​∈X

Equation 4 holds for any n∈Nn \in \mathbb Nn∈N, all finite sequences of points x1,...,xnx_{1},...,x_{n}x1​,...,xn​ in X\mathcal XX and all choices of nnn real-valued coefficients c1,...,cn∈Rc_{1},...,c_{n} \in \mathbb Rc1​,...,cn​∈R. It's the positive definiteness of the kernel function that guarantees the existence of a dot product space F\mathcal FF and a feature map ϕ:X→F\phi:\mathcal X\to \mathcal Fϕ:X→F such that k(x,x′)=⟨ϕ(x),ϕ(x′)⟩Fk(x,x') = \langle \phi(x), \phi(x') \rangle_{\mathcal{F}}k(x,x′)=⟨ϕ(x),ϕ(x′)⟩F​ without the need for the explicit construction of ϕ\phiϕ.

Another important property of a positive definite kernel is that it induces a space of functions from X\mathcal XX to R\mathbb RR called a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space RKHS H\mathcal HH, which is why the p.d. kernel is also called a reproducing kernel.

Two important properties define an RKHS: First, for any x∈Xx \in \mathcal Xx∈X, the function k(x,⋅):y↦k(x,y)k(x,\cdot):y\mapsto k(x,y)k(x,⋅):y↦k(x,y) is an element of H\mathcal HH. So, when kkk is used, the feature space F\mathcal FF is the associated RKHS H\mathcal HH;

k:X→H⊂RXx↦k(x,⋅) k:\mathcal X \to \mathcal H \subset {\mathbb R}^{\mathcal X}\\ x \mapsto k(x,\cdot) k:X→H⊂RXx↦k(x,⋅)

Where RX\mathbb R^{\mathcal X}RX denotes the vector space of functions from X\mathcal XX to R\mathbb RR, we call kkk the canonical feature map.

Second, a function k:X×X→Rk : \mathcal X \times \mathcal X \to \mathbb Rk:X×X→R is called a reproducing kernel of H\mathcal HH if k(⋅,x)∈Hk(\cdot,x) \in \mathcal Hk(⋅,x)∈H for all x∈Xx \in \mathcal Xx∈X and the reproducing property

f(x)=⟨f,k(⋅,x)⟩H f(x) = \langle f,k(\cdot,x) \rangle_{\mathcal H} f(x)=⟨f,k(⋅,x)⟩H​

holds for all f∈Hf \in \mathcal Hf∈H and all x∈Xx \in \mathcal Xx∈X. Important for us: k(x,x′)=⟨k(x,⋅),k(x′,⋅)⟩Hk(x,x')=\langle k(x,\cdot),k(x',\cdot) \rangle_{\mathcal H}k(x,x′)=⟨k(x,⋅),k(x′,⋅)⟩H​.

Aronszajn (1950): “There is a one-to-one correspondance between the reproducing kernel kkk and the RKHS H\mathcal HH”.

Kernel Mean Embedding of Marginal Distributions

So how can we extend this mapping to marginal distributions? We simply take the mapping µµµ which defines the representer in H\mathcal HH of any distribution P\mathbb PP. The distribution P\mathbb PP is transformed into an element, the mean embedding µP{µ}_{\mathbb P}µP​ in an RKHS matching the positive definite kernel kkk. The element is the expected value in the RKHS and since P\mathbb PP is a probability density distribution, it can be written as an integral!

µ:P→HP↦∫k(⋅,x) dPx µ: \mathcal P \to \mathcal H\\ \mathbb P \mapsto \int k(\cdot,x)\, \mathrm{d\mathbb P}x µ:P→HP↦∫k(⋅,x)dPx

Visual representation of this mean embedding:

Now how much information can this mean representation capture about the distribution P\mathbb PP? This depends on the used kernel! This can range from only the first moment of P\mathbb PP to all information of P\mathbb PP.

k(x,x′)=⟨x,x′⟩k(x,x')=\langle x,x' \ranglek(x,x′)=⟨x,x′⟩ : the first moment of P\mathbb PP k(x,x′)=(⟨x,x′⟩+1)pk(x,x')={(\langle x,x' \rangle+1)}^{p}k(x,x′)=(⟨x,x′⟩+1)p (polynomial kernels): moments of P\mathbb PP up to order p∈Np \in \mathbb Np∈N k(x,x′)k(x,x')k(x,x′) is universal/characteristic: all information of P\mathbb PP

A kernel kkk is characteristic if the map

P↦µP \mathbb P \mapsto µ_{\mathbb P} P↦µP​

is injective, i.e., ∥µP−µQ∥H=0{\|µ_{\mathbb P} - µ_{\mathbb Q} \|}_{\mathcal H} = 0∥µP​−µQ​∥H​=0 if and only if P=Q\mathbb P = \mathbb QP=Q. This injectivity of the map P↦µP\mathbb P \mapsto µ_{\mathbb P}P↦µP​ ensures that the RKHS embedding is suitibale for regression problems (each element in the feature space corresponds to one unique distribution in the original space)!

An example of a characteristic kernel, also the kernel used in all toy examples, is the Gaussian kernel, which is part of the class of kernels called radial basis functions (RBFs):

kRBF(x,x′)=exp(−∥x−x′∥22σ2) k^{RBF}(x,x') = exp(-\frac{\|x-x'\|^{2}}{2{\sigma}^{2}}) kRBF(x,x′)=exp(−2σ2∥x−x′∥2​)

with σ>0\sigma > 0σ>0 the bandwith parameter. The Gram matrix of the Gaussian kernel becomes a matrix of ones for σ→∞\sigma \to \inftyσ→∞ and an indentity matrix for σ→0\sigma \to 0σ→0. Which means for the former that all input is the same, and for the latter that all input is completely unique. This RBF kernel is a stationary kernel, which means that it can be described as a function of the difference of its inputs. The RBF kernel is also a universal kernel which means it can represent any smooth function with a high degree of accuracy , assuming chosen the right bandwith parameter. It must be noted that all universal kernels are characteristic, but characteristic kernels may not be universal.

In practive however the access to the true distribution of P\mathbb PP is often lacking, and only an i.i.d. sample x1,...,xnx_{1},...,x_{n}x1​,...,xn​ from P\mathbb PP is avaible. We can estimate µPµ_{\mathbb P}µP​ by

µ^P:=1n∑i=1nk(xi,⋅) \hat{µ}_{\mathbb P} := \frac{1}{n}\displaystyle\sum_{i=1}^{n}k(x_{i}, \cdot) µ^P​:=n1​i=1∑n​k(xi​,⋅)

with µ^P\hat{µ}_{\mathbb P}µ^P​ an unbiased estimate of µP{µ}_{\mathbb P}µP​. add probability mass distribution thingy? It can be estimated consistently

Toy example 1: Inference (using Maximum mean discrepancy)

Now, let's discuss the maximum mean discrepancy with the help of a toy example. First, what is the maximum mean discrepancy or MMD? The MMD corresponds to the RKHS distance between mean embeddings:

MMD2(P,Q,H)=∥µP−µQ∥H2=∥µP∥H−2⟨µP,µQ⟩H+∥µQ∥H. MMD^{2}(\mathbb P, \mathbb Q, \mathcal H) = {\|µ_{\mathbb P} - µ_{\mathbb Q}\|}_{\mathcal H}^{2} = {\|µ_{\mathbb P}\|}_{\mathcal H} -2{\langle µ_{\mathbb P}, µ_{\mathbb Q} \rangle}_{\mathcal H} + {\|µ_{\mathbb Q}\|}_{\mathcal H}. MMD2(P,Q,H)=∥µP​−µQ​∥H2​=∥µP​∥H​−2⟨µP​,µQ​⟩H​+∥µQ​∥H​.

This distance represents the distance between distributions in the input space! So we can use this to find objects in the inpust space which correspond with a specific KME in the feature space. For the empirical MMD, given {xi}i=1n∼P\{x_i\}_{i=1}^n \sim \mathbb P{xi​}i=1n​∼P and {yj}j=1n∼Q\{y_j\}_{j=1}^n \sim \mathbb Q{yj​}j=1n​∼Q:

MMDu2(P,Q,H)=1n(n−1)∑i=1n∑j≠ink(xi,xj)−2nm∑i=1n∑j=1mk(xi,yj)+1m(m−1)∑i=1m∑j≠imk(yi,yj) MMD_u^{2}(\mathbb P, \mathbb Q, \mathcal H) = \frac{1}{n(n-1)}\displaystyle\sum_{i=1}^{n}\displaystyle\sum_{j\neq i}^{n}k(x_{i},x_{j}) - \frac{2}{nm}\displaystyle\sum_{i=1}^{n}\displaystyle\sum_{j = 1}^{m}k(x_{i},y_{j})+ \frac{1}{m(m-1)}\displaystyle\sum_{i=1}^{m}\displaystyle\sum_{j\neq i}^{m}k(y_{i},y_{j}) MMDu2​(P,Q,H)=n(n−1)1​i=1∑n​j=i∑n​k(xi​,xj​)−nm2​i=1∑n​j=1∑m​k(xi​,yj​)+m(m−1)1​i=1∑m​j=i∑m​k(yi​,yj​)

Let's generate some data points which lay in a circle with an unknown radius and add some noise:

Let's consider an arbitrary problem: we want to fit an ideal model to this data, 100 equally spaced points which lay on a certain radius which represents the input data, how could we do this? We can use the MMD!

First, we define our kernel, the Gaussian kernel (an RBF kernel). In this example we are using the KernelFunctions.lj package, which uses ScaleTransfrom, which is the inverse of the lengthscale.

k = SqExponentialKernel() ∘ ScaleTransform(0.1)

Then we generate 100 model circles with different radii ranging from 15 to 25. We compute the Gram matrix over the input data, notice the "RowVecs(X)", this means we take both the x and y coordinate as input, this becomes a value which is then compared with the other input values. For each model circle, we generate the Gram matrix over the model data and the Gram matrix over the model data and input data together. After this we have everything we need to compute the MMD such as in equation 12.

R2 = LinRange(15, 25, n)
	mmds = Array{Float64}(undef, 0, 1)
	K1 = kernelmatrix(k, RowVecs(X))
	for i in 1:n
		A = circle(100, R2[i])
		K2 = kernelmatrix(k, RowVecs(A))
		K3 = kernelmatrix(k, RowVecs(X), RowVecs(A))
		mmd = mean(K1) - 2 * mean(K3) + mean(K2)
		mmds = [mmds; mmd]

Plotting out our results for the MMD versus the radius of each model circle.

Taking the minimum of the MMD and the corresponding Radius.

index = argmin(mmds)
	minimum(mmds)
    R2[index]

Generating our model fit with the found radius which has the matching smallest MMD!

Kernel Mean Embedding of Conditional Distributions

Just like we performed KME on marginal distributions, we can perform KME on conditional distributions, capturing even more complex data!

Let's say we have two positive definite kernels, k:X×X→Rk : \mathcal X \times \mathcal X \to \mathbb Rk:X×X→R and l:Y×Y→Rl : \mathcal Y \times \mathcal Y \to \mathbb Rl:Y×Y→R for the respective domains of XXX and YYY, with respective RKHSs H\mathcal HH and G\mathcal GG. Then, the conditional mean embeddings of the conditional distributions P(Y∣X)\mathbb P(Y|X)P(Y∣X) and P(Y∣X=x)\mathbb P(Y|X=x)P(Y∣X=x) can be defined as UY∣X:H→G\mathcal U_{Y|X}: \mathcal H \to \mathcal GUY∣X​:H→G and UY∣X∈G\mathcal U_{Y|X} \in \mathcal GUY∣X​∈G, they satisfy:

UY∣x=EY∣X[ϕ(Y)∣X=x]=UY∣Xk(x,⋅)EY∣X[g(Y)∣X=x]=⟨g,UY∣x⟩G,∀g∈G. \mathcal U_{Y|x} = \mathbb E_{Y|X}[\phi(Y)|X=x] = \mathcal U_{Y|X}k(x,\cdot)\\ \mathbb E_{Y|X}[g(Y)|X=x] = \langle g,\mathcal U_{Y|x} \rangle_{\mathcal G}, \forall g \in \mathcal G. UY∣x​=EY∣X​[ϕ(Y)∣X=x]=UY∣X​k(x,⋅)EY∣X​[g(Y)∣X=x]=⟨g,UY∣x​⟩G​,∀g∈G.

UY∣X\mathcal U_{Y|X}UY∣X​ is an operator from RKHS H\mathcal HH to G\mathcal GG and UY∣x\mathcal U_{Y|x}UY∣x​ is an element in that RKHS G\mathcal GG. In equation 13 it is important to note that the conditional mean embedding of P(Y∣X=x)\mathbb P(Y|X=x)P(Y∣X=x) is the conditional expectation of the feature map of YYY given that X=xX = xX=x. The operator UY∣X\mathcal U_{Y|X}UY∣X​ is the conditioning operation that when apploef to ϕ(x)∈H\phi(x) \in \mathcal Hϕ(x)∈H delivers the conditional mean embedding UY∣x\mathcal U_{Y|x}UY∣x​. Equation 14 shows the reproducing property of UY∣x\mathcal U_{Y|x}UY∣x​: it should be a representer of conditional expectation in G\mathcal GG with regards to P(Y∣X=x)\mathbb P(Y|X=x)P(Y∣X=x).

Now, let CXX:H→H\mathcal C_{XX}: \mathcal H \to \mathcal HCXX​:H→H and CXY:H→G\mathcal C_{XY}: \mathcal H \to \mathcal GCXY​:H→G be the covariance operator on XXX and corss-covariance operator from XXX to YYY, respectively. Then:

UY∣X:=CXYCXX−1UY∣x:=CXYCXX−1k(x,⋅) \mathcal U_{Y|X} := \mathcal C_{XY}\mathcal C_{XX}^{-1}\\ \mathcal U_{Y|x} := \mathcal C_{XY}\mathcal C_{XX}^{-1}k(x,\cdot) UY∣X​:=CXY​CXX−1​UY∣x​:=CXY​CXX−1​k(x,⋅)

In an infinite RKHS, CXX−1\mathcal C_{XX}^{-1}CXX−1​ does not exist, so we often use a regularised version, that is:

UY∣X:=CXY(CXX+λI)−1, \mathcal U_{Y|X} := \mathcal C_{XY}(\mathcal C_{XX} + \lambda \mathcal I)^{-1}, UY∣X​:=CXY​(CXX​+λI)−1,

Where λ\lambdaλ is the regularization parameter (> 0) and I\mathcal II is the identitiy operator in H\mathcal HH.

In practice: P(X,Y)\mathbb P(X,Y)P(X,Y) is unknown, and CXY\mathcal C_{XY}CXY​ and CXX\mathcal C_{XX}CXX​ cannot be computed directly. To overcome this, consider the i.i.d. sample (x1,y1),...,(xn,yn)(x_{1},y_{1}),...,(x_{n},y_{n})(x1​,y1​),...,(xn​,yn​) from P(X,Y)\mathbb P(X,Y)P(X,Y). Let Y:=[ϕ(x1),...,ϕ(xn)]TY := [\phi(x_{1}),...,\phi(x_{n})]^{T}Y:=[ϕ(x1​),...,ϕ(xn​)]T and Φ:=[φ(y1),...,φ(yn)]T\Phi := [\varphi(y_{1}),...,\varphi(y_{n})]^{T}Φ:=[φ(y1​),...,φ(yn​)]T where ϕ:X→H\phi : \mathcal X \to \mathcal Hϕ:X→H and φ:Y→G\varphi : \mathcal Y \to \mathcal Gφ:Y→G are the feature maps associated with the kernels kkk and lll, respectively. The corresponding Gram matrices are defined as K=YTYK = Y^{T}YK=YTY and L=ΦTΦL = \Phi^{T}\PhiL=ΦTΦ. With this information, the empirical estimator of the conditional mean embedding is given by
